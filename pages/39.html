Gun Boat Diplomacy

‘The East India Company iron steam ship Nemesis, commanded by Lieutenant W. H. Hall, with boats from the Sulphur, Calliope, Larne and Starling, destroying the Chinese war junks in Anson's Bay, on 7 January 1841’, (1843), Edward Duncan. This paper makes clear the technological disparities between the European and Asian naval forces. The Chinese were powerless to resist when the gunboats sailed into their harbours:

  • Also sometimes referred to as Big Stick diplomacy, this very simply is a term that refers to the pursuit of foreign policy objectives through displays of military power and intimidation, typically manifest in superior naval arms. The term originated in the nineteenth century in the era of rapid European imperial expansion, where gunboat diplomacy was frequently employed as European nations used their superior military techniques to gain concessions from less well-equipped states. Frequently, gunboat diplomacy worked largely through intimidation – the threat of violence was often enough to gain the required result.
  • The display of superior military might was intended to allow the imperialists to arrange beneficial economic relationships and expand their territories.

Treaty of Nanking

‘HMS Wellesley and the British Squadron Sailing Under the Walls of Nanking, Saluting the Peace Treaty’, (ca.1840), Rundle Burges Watson. The Treaty brought an end to the First Opium War and imposed exploitative trade conditions on the Chinese. The superiority of ships such as the HMS Wellesley made victory for the British a near certainty:

  • Signed in 1842, this peace treaty brought an end to the First Opium War (1839 – 1842). The primary aim of the treaty was economic; it was designed to remove the strictures imposed on trade by the Canton system. Four new “treaty ports” were opened up, including Shanghai. Fixed tariffs in these ports were also established. Alongside these economic benefits to the British, the British demanded a payment of six million dollars in recompense for the opium that had by confiscated by Lin Zexu in 1839. The treaty also established the cession of Hong Kong from China to Britain. The island now became a crown colony.
  • The articles of the treaty effectively confirmed the military superiority of the European imperialists over the Chinese and facilitated their continued economic exploitation. It is easy to understand why this treaty was the first of what the Chinese would later call the “Unequal Treaties”.

Canton System

Vue du port de Canton (View of the Port of Canton), Sunqua (ca.1830). The Canton system was an attempt by the Chinese to control trade with Europe by directing all external trade to the port of Canton. This is evident from this image where you can see the array of western flags billowing over the harbor, including the British, French, Dutch, and the United States. Note how western in style the buildings in the background are, indicating the pervasive influence of western culture.

  • In operation from the mid-eighteenth century through to the end of the First Opium War (1757 – 1842), the Canton System is the name given to the Chinese system for attempting to control imports. All trade was centered on the southern port city of Canton (modern Guangzhou), and this was done to counter the belief that the country was under threat from external influences.
  • British demand for tea, and a Chinese reluctance to trade in anything other than silver, saw the East India Company begin to sell opium grown on its Indian plantations, which were then shipped into China to be sold for silver. Chinese resistance to this policy ultimately led to the First Opium War. British victory broke the Canton system, with the 1842 Treaty of Nanking forcing open other Chinese ports, such as Shanghai. Although unique to Chinese society, the Canton system provides a clear example of the effects of ‘Gunboat Diplomacy’; a sovereign nation’s economic model was altered, drastically, on the basis of another’s military supremacy.
