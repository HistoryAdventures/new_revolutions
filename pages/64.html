  • While the leaders of these companies continued to grow richer, the rising profits witnessed in many of these industries was not reflected in wage raises for the multitude of employees packed into these factories and warehouses. Rather, many immigrants who had rushed to the cities for work discovered the wages were painfully low while the work was grueling. Many businesses also exploited children. In 1900, child labor was so rampant that nearly 20% of the American workforce was under the age of 16

Muckraking Journalism

In 1906, Upton Sinclair published The Jungle, which ripped the lid off of factory conditions in Chicago. Photo of Upton Sinclair from “Time” Magazine (1934).

  • The wheels of change to do away with this unjust industrial complex began to turn in the closing years of the nineteenth century, as intrepid investigative journalists, known as “muckrakers,” began to research and expose these corrupt practices. By shedding light on these injustices, progressive organizations began to form to improve worker conditions, change hiring practices, and fight for better wages. A law was proposed within the New York state legislature in 1892, for example, to prohibit factory work for children under the age of 15; this law was eventually passed under the Fair Labor Standard Act of 1938. It was this same act that created the concept of a minimum wage.
